You're looking at a product page for a watch you like. Then you hit the spec sheet. *100M WR. NH35. 316L. 20/46mm. Hardlex.* It reads like a parts list for a submarine. This guide translates every term you'll encounter when buying a watch under $1,000, no fluff, just what each number means and whether it changes your buying decision.

---

## "What does the case diameter number actually tell me?"

The case diameter is the width of the watch face measured in millimetres, almost always from the 9 o'clock edge to the 3 o'clock edge, **not** including the crown. When you measure a round watch case, you're measuring the diameter, not including the crown or any pushers. It's the headline size you'll see everywhere: 38mm, 40mm, 42mm.

But diameter alone doesn't tell the whole story. The number that actually determines how a watch *spans* your wrist is the **lug-to-lug** distance, the length from the tip of the top lugs to the tip of the bottom lugs. Lug-to-lug distance is the total span from one end of the case to the other across your wrist, and it's super important for how the watch actually wears, especially on smaller wrists. Two 40mm watches from different brands can feel completely different because one has a compact lug-to-lug of 46mm and the other stretches to 50mm.

As trends and fashion have changed, case diameter has altered drastically over the years; in the 1950s the most popular size of a men's wristwatch was 34mm, compared to the most popular size today being 42mm. That context matters when you're buying vintage-inspired pieces from Orient or Seiko, which often run smaller than their modern-sport counterparts.

## "What's lug width, and do I actually need to know it before buying?"

*This one comes up a lot.*

Yes, and it's the spec most first-time buyers skip, then regret. Lug width is the distance between the two lugs of your watch case, the gap where the strap sits, measured in millimetres. A strap must match this width exactly, or it won't fit.

Common lug widths in the under-$1,000 category are 18mm, 20mm, and 22mm, with 20mm covering the majority of popular references. Measuring the lug width on your watch isn't something to eyeball and estimate. Precision matters for sizing your straps because even a millimetre off could spell disaster for your strap replacement plans.

One subtle trap: lug width and lug-to-lug are **not** the same thing, and the two get confused constantly. People often confuse the lug width with the lug-to-lug. This is notorious with microbrands on forums. A watch has a diameter of 42mm, and potential buyers ask about the lug-to-lug size, and a brand rep answers, "The lug to lug is 20mm." That's wrong. 20mm would be the lug *width*. Lug width is the gap between the horns. Lug-to-lug is the full top-to-bottom case length.

## "Sapphire, mineral, Hardlex, does the crystal really matter?"

*A question we hear often.*

It matters more than most beginners expect. The crystal is the transparent cover over the dial, and there are three types you'll encounter under $1,000:

| Crystal Type | Scratch Resistance | Shatter Resistance | Typical Price Tier |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sapphire** | Excellent (Mohs 9) | Lower, chips on sharp impact | $200+ watches |
| **Mineral** | Moderate | Good | $50, $300 watches |
| **Acrylic (Hesalite)** | Poor, scratches easily | Excellent, polishable | Budget & vintage-style |

Synthetic sapphire (aluminum oxide) ranks 9 on the Mohs hardness scale, second only to diamond, making it virtually scratch-proof under normal wear, but it can shatter on sharp impact. Mineral glass trades some scratch resistance for flexibility, making it less likely to crack if you catch a doorframe. Mineral crystal scratches easily and will look worn within a year of daily use, which is why sapphire is the upgrade worth seeking out even on budget picks.

Many watch enthusiasts mistake sapphire crystal for natural gemstone sapphire, but it's actually a synthetic material, lab-grown aluminum oxide, engineered specifically for durability. When a spec sheet says simply "crystal" or "glass" without specifying, assume mineral.

## "What does the water resistance rating mean, can I actually swim with a 100M watch?"

Water resistance ratings are one of the most misread specs in the entire watch market. The metre figure on the dial or case back is a **static lab pressure test**, not a real-world diving depth. Here's what the numbers translate to in actual use:

| Rating | What It Means in Practice |
|---|---|
| 30M / 3 ATM | Splash-proof only. No swimming. |
| 50M / 5 ATM | Light swimming, no diving, no jumping in. |
| 100M / 10 ATM | Swimming and snorkelling. |
| 200M / 20 ATM | Recreational scuba diving. |
| 300M+ / 30 ATM | Professional diving. |

It's a common misconception that a watch's water resistance is a permanent feature. In reality, this protection relies on a system of rubber or silicone gaskets and seals that create a barrier against moisture. The gaskets around the crown, crystal, and case back can wear out over time and should be inspected regularly.

A screw-down crown is the single most useful water resistance upgrade. Many dive and sports watches feature a screw-down crown, a type that physically screws into the case, compressing the gasket to create a much more secure, airtight seal. If you're buying a watch you plan to swim with, look for both a 100M+ rating **and** a screw-down crown. The rating alone isn't enough.

Water resistance degrades over time as gaskets age, so have your watch pressure-tested every 1, 2 years if you regularly expose it to water.

## "What do the movement codes like NH35 or 9015 mean on a spec page?"

*Readers frequently ask:* what are all these alphanumeric codes in the movement field?

The movement (also called the calibre) is the engine of the watch. The movement, often referred to as the "caliber," powers the hands and complications like chronographs or calendars, and ensures the accurate measurement of time.

In the sub-$1,000 category, you'll see three types listed:

- **Automatic**: Self-winding via a rotor that spins with wrist movement. Needs no battery. Typically accurate to ±10, 20 seconds per day.
- **Manual (hand-wind)**: Mechanical, but you wind the crown yourself, usually every 1, 3 days.
- **Quartz**: Battery-powered. The quartz crystal vibrates at a consistent frequency of 32,768 times per second, driving a small motor that moves the hands, with deviations of only a few seconds per month.

The specific code tells you which manufacturer made the movement. **NH35** and **NH38** are Seiko Instruments automatics, workhorses found in everything from microbrands to the Seiko 5 itself. **Miyota 9015** is Citizen's movement division's premium automatic, known for smoother seconds-hand sweep and a hackable seconds hand. **ETA 2824** is a Swiss automatic that still appears in Tissot and Hamilton references at higher price points within the $1,000 cap.

Mechanical watches, both manual and automatic, need periodic servicing, usually every 3 to 7 years, to keep them running properly. Factor that into your total cost of ownership. A watchmaker service typically runs $75, $150 for a common Japanese calibre.

## "316L stainless steel, PVD, DLC, what do case material codes mean?"

Case material specs tell you what the watch shell is made of and how it's finished. Here's a quick decoder for the terms you'll see most often in the sub-$1,000 market:

| Term | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **316L SS** | Standard watch-grade stainless steel. Corrosion-resistant, hypoallergenic for most people. |
| **904L SS** | Higher-grade steel used by Rolex (and some premium microbrands). Better polish retention. |
| **PVD** | Physical Vapour Deposition, a coating that gives steel a black or gold tone. Can wear through over years. |
| **DLC** | Diamond-Like Carbon coating. Harder and more durable than standard PVD. |
| **Titanium** | Lighter than steel, highly corrosion-resistant. Common on Citizen Promaster sport models. |
| **Hardlex** | Seiko's proprietary mineral crystal variant, harder than standard mineral, softer than sapphire. |

Common stamps on case backs include "Stainless Steel / SS" indicating the case is stainless steel (typically 316L), but "Stainless Steel Back" means only the back plate is steel. The rest of the case may be base metal, brass, or alloy. This distinction matters a lot at the $100, $200 tier where mixed-material cases are common.

Understanding material quality differences, sapphire crystal's permanent scratch resistance versus Hardlex degradation, ceramic bezel fade-proof colouring versus aluminium patina development, empowers value-focused purchasing decisions.

## "Is there a fast way to check all of this before I buy?"

Once you know what each number means, reading a spec sheet takes under two minutes. The practical order is:

1. **Case diameter + lug-to-lug** -> does it fit your wrist?
2. **Lug width** -> can you swap the strap later?
3. **Crystal** -> sapphire or mineral?
4. **Water resistance + crown type** -> does it match how you'll wear it?
5. **Movement code** -> automatic, quartz, or manual? Servicing needs?
6. **Case material** -> solid 316L, or mixed construction?

The gap between a $200 and $500 watch often reflects finishing refinement and brand prestige rather than functional capability, so the specs are your best tool for separating genuine upgrades from logo premium.
