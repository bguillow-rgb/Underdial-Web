Buying a mechanical watch under $1,000 is a great decision. Paying to service one you never needed to touch yet? That's money out the window. Understanding the real rhythm of watch servicing, and the genuine cost attached to it, is one of the most practical things a new collector can learn, and it rarely appears on any spec sheet.

## "So... how often does my automatic watch actually need a service?"

*A question we hear often:* buyers who've just picked up their first Seiko 5 Sports or Tissot Le Locle want to know if there's a schedule they should be following. The short answer: condition beats the calendar every time.

Most manufacturers suggest a full service every four to five years -- but that guideline originated when lubricants degraded faster than modern synthetic oils do. That interval came from an era when lubricants inside mechanical movements degraded more quickly; brands have since significantly improved materials, lubrication, and movement engineering. Real-world practice has shifted accordingly. Many collectors report their watches running accurately for six, seven, or even more years between services when worn regularly and stored well. How often you actually need service depends on how frequently you wear the watch, whether it sees physical activity, and whether it has ever been dropped -- regardless, oils in a movement do dry out over time, creating friction in the metal components.

The smartest approach: check performance, not the date on the receipt.

## "What does it cost to get a Seiko or Tissot serviced?"

A basic service for a watch like a Seiko, Orient, or Tissot with a standard ETA or Miyota movement runs $150--$300 at a reputable independent watchmaker. A full overhaul with parts replacement typically adds up to $200--$400. Those numbers climb with complications: chronograph movements are vastly more complex than time-only counterparts, typically adding 100--200 extra components that are more difficult to disassemble and whose parts wear faster.

Here's the cost reality across the tiers most Underdial users care about:

| Watch Tier | Typical Movement | Basic Service Cost | Full Overhaul |
|---|---|---|---|
| Entry (Seiko 5, Orient Bambino) | NH35, Miyota, in-house | $150--$250 | $200--$350 |
| Mid (Tissot PRX, Hamilton Khaki) | ETA, Powermatic 80 | $200--$350 | $300--$500 |
| Quartz (any brand) | Battery-powered | $10--$50 (battery) | $120--$200 (overhaul) |
| Microbrand (Seiko NH34/NH35 host) | NH35, Miyota 9015 | $150--$300 | $200--$400 |

For watches under $300, service costs may approach or exceed the watch's value -- sometimes replacement makes more economic sense, though sentimental value changes that calculation. This is a genuine ownership consideration worth factoring into your buying decision *before* you buy, not after.

## "What actually happens during a service? Is it worth it?"

*This one comes up a lot:* collectors wonder if a service is just a marketing upsell, or whether something substantive actually happens. It's genuinely substantive.

The process begins with an initial assessment to identify wear or issues; the watch is then disassembled so every movement component can be evaluated and cleaned with specialised solutions. Worn or damaged parts are replaced, the movement is lubricated to cut friction, and the whole thing is reassembled and calibrated for accuracy before the case and bracelet are refurbished. For water-resistant models, the case also undergoes testing to confirm its integrity against moisture and dust.

A mechanical watch has more parts than a car engine and can take just as many hours -- or more -- to service properly. That's why the bill isn't trivial, and why a quote of $150 from a skilled independent isn't cutting corners -- it's simply a different overhead structure than a brand service center.

One often-overlooked point: case polishing is optional and removes metal -- if you don't want it, say so. Excessive polishing rounds off sharp case edges and reduces collector appeal over time, especially on watches with crisp brushed-and-polished finishing.

## "How do I know if my watch actually needs a service right now?"

*Readers frequently ask:* not everyone knows what normal wear looks like versus a genuine red flag. Here are the signs that should prompt action rather than waiting:

- **Shortened power reserve** -- your automatic stops well before its rated hours after a full charge
- **Stiff or gritty crown** -- over time, oils dry up, causing friction in the metal parts and making the crown harder to turn; continuing to move the hands at that stage grinds machined components against each other
- **Accuracy drift beyond normal spec** -- a Seiko NH35 is rated at +45/-35 seconds/day; if it's suddenly off by 3 minutes a day, that's not normal aging
- **Condensation under the crystal** -- moisture inside a watch can lead to rust and corrosion of the movement, requiring immediate servicing to prevent further damage
- **Rattling or grinding** -- excessive spinning or rattling can signal internal wear that won't self-correct

If none of those apply and your watch is running within spec, look for performance indicators rather than blindly following a calendar.

## "Should I go to an authorized service center or find an independent watchmaker?"

This depends entirely on two things: warranty status and movement type.

For watches still under manufacturer warranty, use authorized service to avoid voiding coverage. Most affordable watch brands -- Seiko, Citizen, Tissot -- offer two- to three-year warranties from purchase. Service anything in that window through the brand's official channel.

Once you're out of warranty, the calculus shifts. For non-luxury watches, skilled independent watchmakers provide excellent service at lower cost. The movements in most sub-$1,000 watches -- the NH35, Miyota 9015, ETA 2824 family -- are workhorse calibers that any competent watchmaker services routinely. No proprietary tools or brand-restricted parts involved. If you're on a budget or have a watch out of warranty, a skilled local watch repair shop can offer more personalised and often less expensive service -- just check reviews carefully and confirm they have experience with your movement type.

Authorized service records support resale value better than independent service for luxury brands -- but for a $350 Seiko 5 Sports, the difference in resale impact is negligible. Spend the savings on your next watch.

## "Does a quartz watch need the same kind of servicing?"

No -- and this is one of the underappreciated ownership advantages of a quality quartz piece. A battery swap (commonly $20--$50 depending on brand and whether water-resistance testing is included) handles the vast majority of quartz maintenance needs. A full quartz movement overhaul -- typically including gasket checks, cleaning, and pressure testing -- runs $120--$200 and is usually only necessary every decade or if the watch is running erratically despite a fresh battery.

A quartz watch running slow, fast, or stopping intermittently may need service -- a quartz watch should not exhibit those symptoms unless something is mechanically wrong with the movement itself rather than simply the battery.

For buyers torn between automatic and quartz purely on maintenance economics, quartz wins decisively. The trade-off is the tactile and horological appeal of a mechanical movement -- a personal call, not a financial one. ## "How do I factor service costs into my watch budget?"

*A question we hear often:* first-time buyers treat the sticker price as the full cost. It isn't.

A useful mental model: assume your automatic watch will need a service every five years. At $200--$300 per service, that's roughly $40--$60 per year added to your cost of ownership. On a $400 watch, that's a meaningful percentage. On an $800 watch, it's proportionally lighter. This is one reason mid-range automatics (think Tissot Powermatic 80, Seiko Presage line) can offer better long-term value than bottom-dollar automatics -- preventive maintenance is always cheaper than corrective repair, and a more solid movement tends to need less corrective work over its life.

Service prices vary widely depending on movement complexity, brand policies, parts availability, and how well the watch has been maintained over the years -- staying on top of regular servicing keeps accuracy stable, prevents premature wear, and reduces the risk of costly repairs later.

## Frequently asked questions

**How often should I service an automatic watch?**
Most manufacturers suggest every 4--5 years, but modern lubricants and tightly-toleranced movements mean a well-kept watch that still runs accurately can safely go 6--7 years between services. Let performance guide you more than the calendar.

**How much does it cost to service a Seiko or Orient watch?**
Expect $150--$300 for a basic service at a reputable independent watchmaker. A full overhaul with parts replacement typically runs $200--$400. For watches under $300, service costs can approach the watch's value -- sometimes replacement is the smarter call.

**Do quartz watches need to be serviced?**
Quartz watches need far less maintenance than automatics. A battery swap ($10--$50) is the main recurring cost. A full quartz movement overhaul runs $120--$200 and is only needed every decade or if the watch is misbehaving.

**What are the signs a watch needs servicing?**
Key red flags: losing or gaining well beyond its normal rate, a stiff or gritty crown when winding or setting, a shortened power reserve, condensation under the crystal, or rattling sounds when you shake the watch.

**Should I use an authorized service center or an independent watchmaker?**
For watches still under warranty, use an authorized center. For out-of-warranty watches under $1,000 with common movements (NH35, Miyota, ETA), a skilled independent watchmaker usually delivers comparable quality at meaningfully lower prices.

**Is it worth servicing a cheap watch?**
Not always on pure economics. If the service bill is $200 and the watch retails for $150, replacement is rational. But sentimental value, a discontinued reference, or a first watch with personal significance can absolutely justify the spend -- just go in with clear eyes about the math.
