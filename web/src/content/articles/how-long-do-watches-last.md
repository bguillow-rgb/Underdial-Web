Most people asking this question aren't watch nerds. They're about to spend $200, $500, or $900 on a watch and want to know whether it'll still be on their wrist in ten years. The honest answer is: it depends almost entirely on movement type and how you treat it, not on the price tag.

## "So how long will this watch actually last -- give me a real number"

*A question we hear often:*

The most direct answer, by movement type:

A well-crafted quartz watch can last roughly 20-30 years, and some high-end quartz watches go longer. The movement itself isn't the limiting factor; the battery is a recurring maintenance item. The real failure point is the electronic circuitry. If the main chip or circuit board is damaged in an older movement, and original spare parts are no longer available, repair becomes very difficult or impossible.

A well-maintained automatic, by contrast, can last decades or a lifetime. The key is regular servicing every 3-5 years to clean, lubricate, and replace worn parts. High-quality mechanical watches -- especially from reputable brands -- often run reliably for 50 years or more with consistent care.

The practical takeaway: if you're buying a quartz watch, plan for 20+ reliable years with minimal effort. If you're buying an automatic, the ceiling is effectively unlimited, provided you service it.

## "What actually wears out inside a watch?"

*This one comes up a lot:*

Both movement types share one fundamental enemy: lubrication breakdown. Every component in a movement is oiled to minimize wear, and over time that oil dries out or turns gummy. That's why servicing exists. A watchmaker disassembles the movement, cleans every component, reapplies fresh oils, and replaces anything that shows wear.

Quartz watches have an additional concern that doesn't apply to automatics: electronics age. The circuits in quartz movements degrade over time, and once replacement parts are discontinued, repairs become impossible. This is why vintage quartz watches from the 1970s -- Seiko and Citizen models from that era -- can be tricky to service today even when they look pristine externally. The mechanical parts are fine; the chip is the bottleneck.

For automatics, the wear pattern is different. The movement is made up of many tiny gears, levers, and springs. Metal on metal, no matter how light the contact, will eventually wear. But this takes many years, and a regular service schedule prevents serious damage before it starts. Crucially, every component is physically replaceable by any trained watchmaker -- that's the fundamental longevity advantage of mechanical design.

## "Does the battery replacement schedule actually matter that much for quartz?"

Yes, and most people underestimate this. A battery left too long will eventually leak, corroding the circuit board. In a modern quartz watch with a proprietary movement or a discontinued model, a corroded circuit board can be a fatal injury.

For a standard, time-only analog quartz watch, battery life averages between 2 and 5 years. The exact duration depends on the age of the watch, the movement type, and the size and quality of the battery installed. Watches with complications -- chronographs, backlights, or Bluetooth -- drain batteries faster. The chronograph function is the biggest power consumer: run it for hours a day and battery life can drop to under a year.

The practical rule: change your quartz battery every 2-3 years whether or not the watch has stopped. Don't wait for the four-second jump end-of-life signal, and certainly don't let it sit dead.

## "Are automatic watches under $1,000 built to actually last decades?"

*Readers frequently ask:*

This is where the affordable watch market genuinely earns its reputation. The movements powering most sub-$1,000 automatics -- Seiko's NH35 and 4R36, Miyota's 8215 and 9015, the ETA 2824-2 and its Sellita SW200 equivalent -- are deliberately designed for serviceability. Parts are widely available and will remain so for the foreseeable future. That's not guaranteed for every proprietary luxury calibre at five times the price.

A Seiko Presage running the 4R35 and a Swiss watch running the same ETA base movement your grandfather's watch used have essentially the same long-term serviceability outlook. What varies is finishing quality, accuracy, and decoration, not the core lifespan architecture.

Longevity depends far more on maintenance habits than on the initial price tag. That's worth keeping in mind before you spend.

## "How often does an automatic watch actually need to go in for service?"

Service intervals are one area where advice genuinely varies. The broad consensus among watchmakers: service every 3 to 7 years, depending on usage and conditions. Daily wearers sit toward the shorter end of that range. A watch worn every day is exposed to repeated winding, crown adjustments, humidity, dust, and temperature changes in a way that a dress watch worn twice a year simply isn't.

A watch left in a drawer, though, isn't safely resting. If it sits long enough, the lubrication hardens and the movement struggles to run. The best thing you can do for a mechanical watch you're not wearing regularly is wind it occasionally, and if you own several automatics, consider a watch winder.

For a full strip-down service -- every gear and jewel inspected, worn components replaced, seals renewed -- a 7 to 10 year interval is reasonable. Budget roughly $100-$300 at a qualified independent watchmaker for a standard Japanese or Swiss lever movement.

## "What kills a watch prematurely -- things I should actually avoid?"

| Threat | Automatics | Quartz | Severity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dead battery left inside | N/A | High -- can leak acid onto circuit board | Critical for quartz |
| Magnetic fields | Medium -- disrupts regulation | Low -- minimal effect | Serious for automatics |
| Water ingress | High -- corrodes movement | High -- corrodes circuit | Critical for both |
| Skipping service | High -- dried lubricants cause wear | Low -- less internal friction | Critical for automatics |
| Drops and shocks | Medium -- can dislodge jewels or rotor | Low -- quartz is more shock-tolerant | Worse for automatics |
| Extreme heat/cold | Medium -- degrades lubricants | Medium -- affects battery and crystal stability | Both affected |

The single most common way a decent watch dies young isn't a dramatic impact. It's a dead quartz battery sitting in the case for 18 months, or an automatic that hasn't been serviced in 12 years running on dry bearings. Both are entirely preventable.

As for knowing when your automatic needs attention: the watch gains or loses time despite being fully wound, the hands appear slightly out of alignment, the date function stops working, or there's a subtle grating sound from the rotor. Any of these means a trip to a watchmaker.

## "Does case material affect how long the watch lasts overall?"

More than most people think. Stainless steel is highly resistant to rust, corrosion, and tarnishing, which is why stainless-cased watches regularly survive 50 years of use. Gold-plated cases wear through their plating faster than you'd expect on a daily wearer, exposing the base metal underneath. If longevity is the goal, a solid stainless steel case with a brushed finish is the most forgiving choice.

The bracelet ages too. Keeping links clean and having a watchmaker check for stretch every few years prevents you from needing a full bracelet replacement before the movement ever needs attention.

On the crystal side, upgrading from mineral to sapphire matters for scratch resistance but not for the movement's functional lifespan. A deeply scratched mineral crystal does eventually need replacing, which adds to ownership cost over time, but it won't kill the watch.

## "Will my sub-$1,000 watch hold any resale value if I want to sell it later?"

*Readers frequently ask:*

This is genuinely reassuring news for buyers in this segment. Watches at the $1,000 price point often hold value better than watches at the $3,000-$5,000 range, because the sub-$1,000 segment remains merit-based. Purchases here are driven by genuine demand for what the watch is, not by brand allocation games.

Hamilton watches generally maintain 60-70% of their retail value, particularly limited editions and popular models, which is better than many other Swiss brands under $1,000. Seiko Prospex models with popular calibres show similarly resilient secondary market pricing.

That said, longevity and resale value aren't the same thing. A Seiko 5 Sports may hold 55% of its value at resale while still running perfectly for 30 years. Longevity is about durability; resale is about desirability. Both matter, but if you're buying to keep -- which is the point of a good watch -- prioritise longevity.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long does a quartz watch last?**
A well-made quartz watch typically lasts 20-30 years. The movement itself is durable, but batteries need replacing every 2-5 years. The long-term weak point is the electronic circuit board -- once proprietary chips become unavailable, repairs may become impossible.

**How long does an automatic watch last?**
An automatic watch can last a lifetime -- often 50 years or more -- with proper servicing every 3-7 years. Unlike quartz, every part of a mechanical movement can be cleaned, lubricated, and replaced by a skilled watchmaker, so there's no hard expiry date.

**Do cheaper watches last as long as expensive ones?**
Often, yes -- if the movement is serviceable. A Seiko NH35-powered watch under $300 uses the same basic service model as many $3,000 Swiss watches. Longevity depends more on movement quality and access to parts than on retail price.

**What kills a watch faster than anything else?**
Water ingress is the most common culprit, followed by neglected servicing (dried lubricants cause accelerated wear), strong magnetic fields, and a dead quartz battery left to leak inside the case. None of these are hard to avoid with basic care.

**How do I know if my watch needs servicing?**
Key signs: the watch is losing or gaining noticeably more time than usual, it stops before its rated power reserve is exhausted, you hear an unusual grating or rattling from the rotor, or the crown feels gritty. Any of these warrants a visit to a watchmaker.
