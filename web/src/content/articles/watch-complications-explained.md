Watch listings throw terms like "GMT," "chronograph," and "moonphase" at you constantly, often without any explanation of what they actually do, whether you'll use them, or what the service bill looks like in five years. This guide untangles every complication you're likely to encounter under $1,000, tells you which ones earn their keep and which are mostly marketing, and flags where to be careful.

## Wait, what exactly is a "complication" anyway?

A complication is any extra functionality on a watch other than just telling the time, meaning hours, minutes, and seconds. The word sounds intimidating, but the logic is simple: additional information like the date being displayed on a watch requires additional parts and makes the movement more complicated to build and service for a watchmaker, thus, it is called a "complication."

They range from the prosaic to the spectacular. Watch complications can be as simple as a date in a window or as complex as a split-seconds chronograph or minute repeater. For buyers working under $1,000, the relevant ones are a much shorter list, and knowing which to seek out (and which to sidestep) is genuinely useful before you tap "buy."

## Is a date window really a complication, or is every watch just called that now?

*A question we hear often:* yes, a date display is a true complication. It's a display of the date via a numbered disk that passes under an aperture in the dial, usually positioned at 3 o'clock, 4:30, or 6 o'clock, and unless the watch is an annual or perpetual calendar, this date will need to be manually adjusted forward at the end of any month shorter than 31 days.

The date complication is the most widespread feature in this price range, and for good reason. The date complication is the most common and useful feature in modern watches, displaying the current date through a small window on the dial. A day-date variant adds the full day of the week alongside it, which is handy for anyone who loses track of weekdays.

One practical note: a quick-set date function (where you advance the date by pulling the crown to a specific position, without running the time forward) is worth checking for. It's standard on most watches at $200 and up but occasionally absent on budget pieces.

## What does a GMT complication actually do, and do I need one?

A GMT complication adds a fourth hand, usually arrow-tipped or differently coloured, that completes one revolution every 24 hours instead of every 12. A GMT watch allows you to track two time zones at once, usually through an extra hour hand and a 24-hour scale.

Two main types are worth knowing at this price point:

- **True GMT (independent hour hand):** The main hour hand and GMT hand adjust independently, so you can set local time to a new zone without touching the running movement. Better for frequent travellers.
- **Offset GMT (jumps in 1-hour increments):** The hour hand snaps forward or back in one-hour steps. Simpler mechanically and more common under $500.

For many buyers, a GMT is the perfect all-around complication. If you work remotely with colleagues in other time zones, or travel even a few times a year, a GMT earns its place every single day. Solid examples under $1,000 include the Seiko SSK series, the Tissot PRX GMT, and the Orient Star GMT, all running proven Japanese or Swiss movements without exotic servicing requirements. ## What about chronographs, are they actually useful or just visual noise?

*This one comes up a lot.* A chronograph is a built-in stopwatch. Chronographs stand out from other watches with their trademark pushers that usually flank the crown, generally one starts/stops the chronograph timing, and the other resets the timer back to zero.

Honestly: most people who buy a chronograph use the stopwatch function far less than they expect. The real reasons chronographs sell are the busy, purposeful dial layout and the satisfying click of the pushers. Both are legitimate reasons to buy one, just be clear-eyed about it.

Where you want to be careful under $1,000 is with mechanical vs. meca-quartz chronographs:

- **Meca-quartz** (like Seiko's VK63/VK64 or Miyota's 1L45) uses a quartz base movement with a mechanical module on top. Battery-powered accuracy, smooth sweeping chrono hand, service cost close to a standard quartz. Excellent value under $400.
- **Fully mechanical** chronograph movements (ETA Valjoux 7750, Miyota 9S40, Seiko 8R series) are more traditional but significantly more expensive to service. A full chronograph overhaul can run $400-$600 at a competent watchmaker, roughly double the cost of a simple three-hand movement. Factor that into your five-year ownership cost before you buy.

Chronographs range from affordable quartz models to highly collectible mechanical movements, and the quality difference within each category is wide. Seiko's Prospex Speedtimer, the Tissot T-Race, and Hamilton's Intra-Matic are among the most reliable mechanical chronographs under $1,000.

## How do I think about moonphase, power reserve, and other "character" complications?

Some complications exist more for visual poetry than daily utility, and there's nothing wrong with that.

**Moonphase:** A moonphase complication is not particularly accurate, or useful, but it is stylish and adds nice touches of colour and additional interest and motion to the watch face, making it popular among collectors. Though often associated with expensive, high horology, moonphase complications are also available in more affordable entry-level mechanical watches. The Tissot Carson Premium Moonphase and the Frederique Constant Moonphase both sit under $1,000 and are competent starting points. The key question is whether the moonphase disc is accurately calibrated. Entry-level examples can drift by a day every couple of years, while better-regulated ones stay accurate for decades.

**Power reserve indicator:** Not covered here in full (see our [watch power reserve explained](https://underdial.com/articles/watch-power-reserve-explained) guide), but as a complication it shows you how much mainspring tension remains before the watch stops. Genuinely useful on manually-wound watches, and less critical on automatics you wear daily.

**Tourbillon under $1,000:** Avoid. A real tourbillon, a complication that counteracts the effects of gravity on a mechanical movement by placing the escapement inside a rotating cage, requires enormous skill to build and legitimately costs tens of thousands of dollars at minimum. Anything marketed as a tourbillon under $1,000 is either a decorative display with no actual cage rotation or an extremely low-quality movement with poor accuracy. It is one of the most common traps in online watch shopping.

## Does adding complications make a watch cost more to service?

*Readers frequently ask:* yes, and it's a cost that's easy to overlook when you're excited about a dial.

More complications mean more moving parts, which can require more servicing. A simple three-hand automatic movement service from a competent independent watchmaker typically runs $150-$250. Add a date complication and you're broadly in the same range. Add a mechanical chronograph and you're looking at $400-$600 or more, because the watchmaker must disassemble, clean, and reassemble a significantly more complex module, often with harder-to-source parts.

This matters more in the sub-$1,000 category than at higher price points, because the service cost is a much larger share of the watch's value. A $500 watch that costs $500 every eight years to service has a very different total cost of ownership than one that costs $150. When Underdial audits a watch against your collection and budget, it's worth mentally adding that long-term servicing reality to the sticker price.

On the flip side, watches running widely used calibers are a safer bet. Most watchmakers can work with ETA movements; in fact, they probably practiced on them during their training, and spare parts are readily available and not limited to certified service centers. The same is broadly true of Seiko NH-series and Miyota movements. A watch using an obscure proprietary module is a riskier long-term proposition under $1,000, because you're more dependent on the brand's own service network.

## So which complication should I actually buy first?

If you have no complication at all on your current watch, or are buying your first proper mechanical, start with a date. It's genuinely useful, adds minimal service complexity, and appears on hundreds of excellent watches across every style from diver to dress.

If you travel, or regularly work across time zones, go straight to a GMT. It becomes a permanent part of how you use the watch in a way a chronograph rarely does for most wearers.

Chronographs are for people who specifically love the look of a busy dial or have an actual use case for timing things. Buy one because you want one, not because you think you'll use the stopwatch constantly.

Moonphases, power reserve displays, and other character complications are personal. Buy them when you find a specific watch you genuinely love, not as a category to hunt down.
