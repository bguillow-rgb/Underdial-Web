When you're comparing two watches and one spec sheet says "±10 s/d" while the other says "-4/+6 s/d COSC," it's easy to glaze over. What does any of it actually mean, and does it change which watch you should buy? This guide answers exactly that, in plain language.

## What does "seconds per day" even mean for a watch?

Watch accuracy is expressed as a rate deviation: how many seconds the movement gains or loses over 24 hours compared to a perfect reference clock. A positive deviation means the watch runs fast; a negative one means it runs slow.

In practical terms, a watch rated at +10 s/d will be roughly 70 seconds fast after one week. Reset it Sunday night and by the following Sunday it's already over a minute out. Whether that bothers you depends entirely on your use case, but knowing what a spec sheet number represents helps you set realistic expectations before you buy.

## What accuracy should I expect from an affordable automatic watch?

*A question we hear often:* the spec sheet on a Seiko or Orient says ±25 s/d, but owners online report much better. Who's right?

Both, actually. Manufacturers publish worst-case tolerances, the envelope their movement must stay within to pass quality control. In real-world use, a Seiko NH35 or Miyota 9015 will often settle to ±5, 10 s/d once it's broken in and worn regularly, even though the stated spec is looser. A deviation of -10 to +20 seconds per day is generally considered acceptable for a modern mechanical watch.

Unlike quartz, mechanical watches rely on gears, springs, and escapements, and factors like temperature, position, magnetism, and daily wear all push the rate around. A watch that runs at +8 s/d in summer may drift to +14 s/d in winter because the hairspring's elasticity changes with temperature. That's not a defect. It's the physics of mechanical timekeeping.

## How does quartz accuracy compare?

The gap is enormous, and worth understanding. A standard quartz movement, the kind in a $50 Seiko 5 quartz or a $200 Tissot Quartz, loses or gains roughly ±15 seconds per month. That works out to about ±0.5 s/d, which is 20 to 50 times more accurate than a typical automatic.

At the top end of quartz precision, METAS certification (used by Omega) pushes movements through criteria including resistance to 15,000 gauss of magnetism and accuracy measured both fully wound and close to stopping, though that applies to movements well above the $1,000 mark. For buyers shopping at more grounded prices, the relevant benchmark is the Seiko 9F caliber, rated to ±10 seconds per year. Effectively set-and-forget for a decade.

The honest takeaway: if raw timekeeping precision matters to you, a good quartz under $500 beats any mechanical under $1,000 on pure accuracy. But most buyers of mechanical watches aren't buying for accuracy. They're buying for the experience of wearing one.

## What is a COSC chronometer, and is it worth paying extra for?

*This one comes up a lot:* "chronometer" sounds impressive, but is it just a marketing label?

It's a real, independently verified standard. COSC (Contrôle Officiel Suisse des Chronomètres) is the Official Swiss Chronometer Testing Institute, and its job is to measure and certify the accuracy of mechanical watch movements to within +6/-4 seconds per day. To earn certification, a movement must pass a 15-day testing period across multiple positions and temperatures, not just dial-up on a bench.

Only about six percent of Swiss watches produced are COSC certified, which makes the label genuinely selective. But there's an important nuance for buyers under $1,000: a non-certified watch can still run within those tolerances, but that tends to be luck rather than deliberate fine-tuning. Certification isn't proof the watch is more accurate than every non-certified watch. It's proof the movement was specifically prepared and adjusted to hit that standard consistently.

| Accuracy standard | Typical deviation | Who uses it | Entry price approx. |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard automatic (e.g. NH35) | ±10, 25 s/d | Seiko, Orient, microbrands | ~$150, $400 |
| ETA / Sellita regulated | ±7, 10 s/d | Tissot, Mido, Hamilton | ~$400, $800 |
| COSC Chronometer | -4/+6 s/d | Tissot Ballade, Mido Commander | ~$600, $900 |
| Rolex Superlative Chronometer | ±2 s/d | Rolex only | $5,700+ |
| Standard quartz | ±0.5 s/d | Seiko, Casio, Citizen | $50, $500 |
| High-accuracy quartz (HAQ) | ±10 s/year | Seiko 9F series | $400, $800 |

COSC-grade versions of ETA movements use upgraded shock-protection systems, hairsprings, and mainsprings compared to their standard-grade counterparts. So the real argument for a chronometer isn't that you'll never reset your watch. It's that the movement internals are a step up in quality and the adjustment work has already been done.

## Does beat frequency (BPH) affect accuracy?

*Readers frequently ask:* "I see 28,800 BPH on one watch and 21,600 BPH on another. Which keeps better time?"

Beat frequency is how fast a movement's balance wheel oscillates. A higher beat rate means more oscillations per second, so each "tick" divides time into smaller increments. In theory that improves accuracy and produces a smoother seconds hand sweep.

In practice under $1,000, the difference is marginal. A well-regulated 21,600 BPH movement (like the hand-wind caliber in the Hamilton Khaki Field Mechanical) can easily outperform a poorly regulated 28,800 BPH movement. Beat frequency matters more for consistency across positions and resistance to shock, since a faster-beating balance wheel is harder to knock off rate, but it's one factor among many. Focus on whether the movement has been adjusted to a specific accuracy rating rather than chasing BPH numbers alone.

## What affects accuracy in daily wear, and what can I do about it?

Temperature changes, mainspring tension, lubrication degradation, impacts, magnetism, and the position the watch is stored or worn in all push the rate around. Here's how each plays out for a typical under-$1,000 automatic.

**Magnetism** is the biggest silent killer. Everyday objects, phone speakers, bag clasps, laptop covers, can magnetize a hairspring and cause the watch to run several minutes fast per day. If your watch suddenly jumps from +8 s/d to +45 s/d, magnetism is the first suspect. A watchmaker can demagnetize a movement in under a minute for a few dollars.

**Position** matters overnight. Leaving the watch lying flat, face up, is generally the safest bet. Gravity pulls the hairspring differently depending on orientation, and a movement adjusted for flat storage will rate differently if left on its side.

**Lubrication age** is a slower drift. As service intervals stretch past the recommended window (typically 3-5 years for an automatic), oils dry out, friction increases, and rate deviation widens. If a watch that used to run at +7 s/d is now at +20 s/d with no other explanation, it may be time for a service.

## So does accuracy actually matter when buying under $1,000?

Honestly, for most buyers: less than you'd think. A watch running at +10 s/d needs a 30-second reset once a week, about the same time it takes to fill a kettle. What matters more is consistency. A watch that reliably runs at +10 s/d every day is far more wearable than one that swings between +3 s/d and +22 s/d depending on how you slept.

At the $1,000 price point, buyers get access to well-regulated movements that perform significantly better than their stated tolerances. A $600 Tissot keeps time about as well as a $3,000 Tudor, which tells you that chasing accuracy through price alone hits diminishing returns well before you reach four figures.

The more useful pre-purchase question isn't "what's the accuracy rating?" It's "is this movement known to be consistent?" ## FAQs

**What is a good accuracy rating for an automatic watch under $1,000?**
±10 seconds per day is considered excellent for an automatic at this price. The official manufacturer spec is often ±10, 25 s/d for movements like the NH35 or Miyota 9015. Anything consistently within ±5 s/d is exceptional for a non-COSC movement.

**What does COSC certified mean on a watch?**
COSC (Contrôle Officiel Suisse des Chronomètres) is an independent Swiss institute that tests mechanical movements over 15 days across multiple positions and temperatures. A certified movement must run within -4/+6 seconds per day to earn the "Chronometer" designation.

**Is a chronometer watch worth it under $1,000?**
Rarely for pure timekeeping. Your phone is more accurate. The value is that COSC-grade movements use finer components and adjustment, meaning better long-term stability. Tissot's Ballade COSC sits under $700 and is one of the few genuinely affordable entry points.

**Does watch position affect accuracy?**
Yes. Gravity pulls the hairspring differently depending on whether the watch is dial-up, dial-down, or crown-up. An automatic left dial-up overnight will often rate differently than one worn on the wrist all day. Position variance is one reason COSC tests movements in six positions.

**How accurate is a quartz watch compared to an automatic?**
A standard quartz watch typically drifts only ±15 seconds per month, roughly 1,500 times more accurate than a typical automatic. High-accuracy quartz (HAQ) movements like Seiko's 9F caliber are rated to within ±10 seconds per year.
