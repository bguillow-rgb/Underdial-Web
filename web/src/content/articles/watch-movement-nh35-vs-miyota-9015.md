Every affordable automatic watch spec sheet eventually throws two names at you: **NH35** and **Miyota 9015**. They're the twin engines of the sub-$1,000 automatic world, and understanding what a brand's choice says about the watch you're considering is one of the most useful things a buyer can learn. This is a plain-English breakdown of what actually matters when these two movements go head to head.

---

## "What even are these movements, and why do I keep seeing them?"

*A question we hear often:* if you've been shopping for an automatic watch under $1,000, you've seen these names everywhere.

When shopping for an affordable automatic watch, you'll almost always see one of two movements inside: a Seiko NH series movement or a Miyota automatic movement. They're the workhorses of the entry-level mechanical world, powering countless microbrands, homages, and even some big-name watches.

These two Japanese giants power 80%+ of sub-$500 automatic watches. Knowing them isn't just enthusiast trivia, it's practical buyer knowledge. When a brand lists "NH35" or "Miyota 9015" in the specs, they're signaling exactly where the product sits in the market and what trade-offs they made to hit that price point.

A movement, or caliber, is the engine of your watch. It controls every function: timekeeping, date change, winding, and power reserve. Both the NH35 and the 9015 are automatic (self-winding) mechanical movements made in Japan, and both include hacking seconds and hand-winding. After that, the similarities start to diverge in ways that genuinely matter on your wrist.

---

## "What are the actual specs, NH35 vs Miyota 9015 side by side?"

Before we get into what any of this *means*, here's the hard data:

| Spec | Seiko NH35 | Miyota 9015 |
|---|---|---|
| Origin | Japan (SII / TMI) | Japan (Citizen / Miyota) |
| Beat rate | 21,600 bph (3 Hz) | 28,800 bph (4 Hz) |
| Jewels | 24 | 24 |
| Movement thickness | 5.32 mm | 3.90 mm |
| Factory accuracy | −20 to +40 s/day | −10 to +30 s/day |
| Power reserve | ~41 hours | ~42 hours |
| Winding direction | Bi-directional (Magic Lever) | Uni-directional |
| Hacking | Yes | Yes |
| Hand-winding | Yes | Yes |
| Movement cost (bare) | ~$35, 45 | ~$100, 135 |
| Finishing | Utilitarian | Perlage + Cotes de Geneve |

The NH35 prioritizes affordability, parts availability, and modding ecosystem at $35, 45 with a 21,600 bph beat rate and 5.32mm thickness, making it the dominant choice for custom watch builders and budget-conscious buyers. The Miyota 9015 delivers superior technical specifications at $100, 135 with a 28,800 bph beat rate, 3.9mm thickness, and better factory accuracy spec.

---

## "Does the seconds hand really look different between these two movements?"

*This one comes up a lot:* yes, it actually does, and it's one of the more noticeable differences in daily wear.

The Seiko NH35 runs at 21,600 bph, a standard sweep, while the Miyota 9015 beats at 28,800 bph, producing a silky, ETA-like smooth sweep. In plain terms: at 21,600 bph the seconds hand advances in six distinct steps per second. At 28,800 bph it makes eight, which to most eyes reads as a continuous glide.

When a brand moves from NH35 to 9015, the smoother sweep is often the first thing buyers notice even if they can't articulate what changed. It's doing quiet work for the perceived quality of the entire watch. There's a reason brands that want to position themselves above the budget tier make this exact switch, the seconds hand is the most constantly visible moving part on the watch, and making it look more refined has an outsized impact on the overall impression.

If you're buying a tool watch or field watch, something you check the time on at a job site rather than a boardroom, this difference is genuinely irrelevant. If the sweep of the seconds hand is part of what you enjoy about wearing a mechanical watch, it matters more than almost any other spec on this list.

## "Which movement is more accurate in real life?"

The Miyota 9015 is generally more accurate out of the box due to its higher beat rate and tighter factory tolerances. However, the Seiko NH35 is often easier for local watchmakers to regulate to near-perfect time.

The NH35's rated accuracy of −20 to +40 s/day looks alarming on paper, but it's a manufacturing ceiling, not a typical performance description. The −20 to +40 s/day rating is not a performance description, it is a manufacturing tolerance ceiling, and most movements run significantly better than that figure suggests. A regulated NH35 performs in the same practical range as Swiss movements that carry a much more flattering rated spec.

In practice, both movements regularly outperform their factory specs, plenty of NH35s run under 10 seconds a day once settled in, and plenty of 9015s do the same. The 9015's higher beat rate gives it a slight theoretical stability advantage across different wearing positions, but you'd need a timegrapher and a spreadsheet to prove it matters in daily wear. Both can be regulated by any competent watchmaker to perform well beyond their factory ratings.

Bottom line: if a watch runs significantly outside ±15 s/day after a few weeks of wear, the problem is usually regulation or a bad individual unit, not an inherent flaw in either movement family.

## "Why does my Miyota watch make a weird spinning noise when I move my wrist?"

*Readers frequently ask:* that sound is the rotor, and it's a real difference between these two movements.

The Miyota 8-series and 9-series use a unidirectional winding system, the rotor spins freely in one direction without resistance, which can create a noticeable "whirring" sound. Many enthusiasts actually enjoy this as a "heartbeat" of the mechanical movement.

The NH35, by contrast, uses Seiko's proprietary Magic Lever system. The NH35's value proposition includes hacking seconds, hand-winding, Magic Lever bi-directional winding, Diashock protection, and a movement price of around $50. Bi-directional winding means the rotor winds the mainspring on both left and right rotations, more efficient, and quieter. Worth factoring in if you work in a quiet office. For active daily wear, the rotor sound is barely noticeable.

## "What's the deal with the thickness difference, does it really matter?"

For many buyers, this is the single most consequential spec in the comparison.

The 9015 is 3.90mm thick. The NH35 is 5.32mm. That's 1.42mm, and it's the single most important spec in this entire comparison.

At 3.9mm thickness, the Miyota 9015 ranks among the slimmest automatic movements available at its price point. This 1.42mm advantage over the NH35 (27% reduction) enables watchmakers to create 9, 11mm dress watches that slide comfortably under shirt cuffs, unachievable with the NH35's 5.32mm height.

For a dive watch or field watch, where a slightly taller case is expected, this difference doesn't matter. For a dress watch or a slim everyday piece that needs to disappear under a cuff, the 9015 isn't just better, it's the *only* option between these two that actually enables that silhouette. Seiko NH movements are thicker, which means watches using them often end up chunkier. The Miyota 9000 series is much thinner, allowing for sleeker designs and slimmer divers and dress pieces.

## "If the 9015 is better, why do so many good watches still use the NH35?"

*A question we hear often:* because "better" is completely context-dependent, and the NH35 wins on criteria that matter enormously at lower price points.

Swiss movements like ETA and Sellita come at a significant premium. Using a Swiss movement in a watch under $500 often means the case, crystal, and finishing take a hit. The same logic applies to the 9015 vs NH35 comparison. When a brand uses an NH35 in a $250 watch, the money saved on the movement goes toward a better case finish, sapphire crystal, or solid end links on the bracelet, all things you *see and feel* every day.

If the watch costs under ~£250 (roughly $300), the NH35 is the right movement and the brand made the smart call. The savings go toward making the watch better where you can actually see it.

There's also the aftermarket angle. The NH35's dominance in custom watch building created a self-reinforcing ecosystem, suppliers produce thousands of aftermarket dials, hands, cases, and bezels specifically for NH35 fitment. This parts availability makes the NH35 the default movement choice for custom builds unless specific requirements justify alternatives. For buyers who enjoy the tinkering side of the hobby, that parts availability has real value.

The 9015 earns its extra cost from around the $300, $500 mark, where brands can absorb the higher movement price without gutting the rest of the watch's spec. In 2026, the best Miyota watches represent some of the strongest value propositions in the $150, $500 price range, and the brands building with them are among the most interesting independents in the hobby.

## "What about Swiss movements like the ETA 2824 or Sellita SW200, how do they fit in?"

Once you've sorted the Japanese tier, you'll encounter Swiss movements at higher price points. The short version: the landscape changed significantly when ETA restricted sales.

Today, ETA movements are rarely available to independent watchmakers, as supply is largely reserved for brands within the Swatch Group. Because of this, most microbrands comparing themselves to ETA are actually using Sellita SW200, a Swiss movement based on the same architecture as the 2824.

Sellita followed up with the SW200-2 Power+ in early 2026, boosting power reserve to 65 hours while keeping full dimensional compatibility. The SW200-1 is a 25.6mm, 26-jewel automatic running at 28,800 vph with 38 to 41 hours of power reserve. Like the 9015, it runs at 28,800 bph, so the seconds sweep is equally smooth. The main difference is the "Swiss Made" designation and a higher price tag that reflects it, not a meaningful performance advantage over a well-regulated 9015 in daily wear.

The SW200 now powers watches from Oris, Christopher Ward, Tag Heuer, and dozens more, brands that need the Swiss Made label as part of their value proposition. For a buyer focused purely on what happens on the wrist under $1,000, that label is largely a marketing premium.

## "So which one should I actually buy?"

Use this as your decision framework:

**Choose a watch with the NH35 if:**
- The watch is priced under ~$300
- It's a tool watch (diver, field, sport) where thickness isn't a concern
- You want the most robust, service-friendly movement possible
- You enjoy the modding or customisation side of the hobby
- Rotor noise bothers you

**Choose a watch with the Miyota 9015 if:**
- The watch is priced $300, $800
- You want a dress watch or a slim everyday watch
- The sweep of the seconds hand matters to you aesthetically
- The watch has an exhibition caseback and you want the movement to look the part
- You want the best out-of-box accuracy in the Japanese tier

The Miyota 9015 leads in raw accuracy, while the NH35 remains impressively reliable for daily wear. The 8215 is acceptable but slightly less refined.

## "Does the movement name on the spec sheet always mean exactly that movement?"

*This one comes up a lot:* not always, and it's worth understanding why.

Watches that use one of these manufacturers' movements often rename and modify them to appear as if they were made in-house, most commonly by embossing their logo onto the rotor. A brand might call their caliber "Caliber 5012" while it's a lightly modified NH35 underneath, or label it "Caliber XYZ" when it's a 9015 with a branded rotor. This isn't deceptive, it's standard practice across the industry, but it means the spec sheet may require a little digging.

The NH35 is essentially the 4R35 without a Seiko stamp, the latter is used as an in-house mechanism, while the former is manufactured for use by other brands. Similarly, Miyota is a movement manufacturer owned by Citizen Watch Co., one of Japan's largest watchmaking groups. Based in Miyota-machi, Nagano Prefecture, the factory produces millions of movements per year. Despite being less talked about than Swiss movement makers, Miyota is arguably more influential in the watch world measured by volume.

If a brand doesn't disclose the base caliber, search the model on watch forums. The community almost always has the answer within the first few posts.
