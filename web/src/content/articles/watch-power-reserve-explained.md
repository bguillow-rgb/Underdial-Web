Power reserve is one of those specs that sounds technical but turns out to matter a lot in day-to-day life, especially once you own more than one automatic watch. Here is everything you need to know before it affects a purchase you are about to make.

## What exactly is power reserve, and why should I care?

*A question we hear often:* buyers see "42h power reserve" in a spec sheet and have no idea whether that's impressive, average, or a red flag.

Think of power reserve as your watch's fuel tank. Power reserve is the amount of stored energy inside a mechanical watch -- the amount of time it can continue to run -- before running out of power. The moment you put an automatic watch down for the night, it starts drawing on that reserve. When the tank hits zero, the watch stops and needs to be reset.

Why does it matter practically? Depending on the power reserve, a watch can be worn for some time, taken off for a day, and picked right back up with the time still running. If you take the watch off on a Friday night and don't pick it up until Monday morning, you may have to adjust the time -- or you may not -- depending on the power reserve of that watch. That Friday-to-Monday scenario is the single most useful mental model for evaluating any power reserve number.

Note that this is purely a mechanical-watch concept. The power reserve, which of course doesn't feature on battery-powered watches, is the functioning time a watch has from the moment it's fully wound until the moment it runs out of energy.

## How does the mainspring actually store and release energy?

All mechanical watches, whether self-winding or manual-winding, are powered by a mainspring that, when wound, coils tightly. That spring is placed inside a barrel, which is where the energy is stored. Once fully wound, the spring begins to unwind in a consistent manner, releasing constant energy to move the wheels, hands, date, and any other functions.

The component that turns that raw spring tension into precise timekeeping is the escapement. The escapement lets the mainspring's power "escape" one tiny tick at a time, which is what moves the gears and, ultimately, the hands on the dial.

On an automatic watch, your wrist keeps topping up this tank throughout the day. An automatic timepiece needs to be worn for about 10-15 hours before it is fully wound. So a desk worker who barely moves their arm may find their watch running low by evening -- a good reason to give it a few manual winds via the crown when that's the case.

## What power reserve numbers should I actually look for under $1,000?

*This one comes up a lot:* spec sheets throw out numbers from 38 hours to 80 hours, and it isn't obvious how much difference that makes in the real world.

Here's a practical breakdown:

| Reserve | Typical Movement | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| 38-42 h | Seiko NH35, ETA 2824, Miyota 8215 | Single-watch daily wearers |
| 44-48 h | Miyota 9015, Sellita SW200 | Daily wear, one night off wrist |
| 70-80 h | Tissot Powermatic 80, Miyota 9039 | Two-watch rotators, weekend travelers |
| 100+ h | Some in-house microbrands | Multi-watch collectors |

The average automatic watch is usually endowed with approximately 36 to 48 hours of power reserve. That's enough for most single-watch owners -- but the moment you start rotating between two pieces, that number starts to sting.

A concrete real-world example: a "weekend-proof" runtime of 70 hours or more means you can take your watch off Friday evening and pick it up Monday morning without having to reset the time and date. For a collector with a Monday-to-Friday daily driver and a weekend piece, 70+ hours on both watches eliminates a surprising amount of friction.

Among affordable brands, Tissot's Powermatic 80 movement is the standout value story at this spec level. It's a modified ETA architecture running at a lower beat rate to stretch the mainspring's energy further, and it's fitted across the PRX Powermatic 80, the Le Locle, and several other models well under $1,000. At the $1,000 price point, buyers gain access to complications and movements previously reserved for four and five-figure watches. An automatic movement with an 80-hour power reserve -- technology that seemed exotic and expensive just years ago -- is now standard at $700-$900 price levels.

## Does a longer power reserve mean the movement is higher quality?

*Readers frequently ask:* whether chasing the biggest reserve number is always the right call when comparing two watches at the same price.

The honest answer is no, and this is where a lot of first-time buyers get misled. It's important to note that the power reserve is not a static figure. It can vary depending on the watch's movement, the number of complications, and how often the watch is worn or wound. A watch with a high power reserve is not necessarily better than one with a lower power reserve -- it simply means it can run for longer without being wound.

In fact, extending power reserve often involves a trade-off: running at a lower beat rate (fewer vibrations per hour) can slightly reduce a movement's resistance to positional errors. That's one reason COSC-certified chronometers tend to run at 28,800 vph rather than the slower 21,600 vph used in some high-reserve movements. The Seiko NH35, for instance, tops out at around 41 hours -- a figure enthusiasts consistently defend as entirely sufficient for a daily-wear workhorse, and they're not wrong.

The better frame: if you enjoy mechanical watches, choose an automatic with a power reserve that fits your wearing routine. Power reserve is not a status feature. It's a practical detail that affects how often you interact with your watch.

## What is a power reserve indicator, and do I actually need one?

A power reserve indicator is a sub-dial or arc on the watch face that acts like a fuel gauge, showing how much energy remains in the mainspring. A complication known as the "power reserve indicator" exists and is usually displayed on the face of a watch to indicate precisely how much life your watch has left in it, similar to a battery or petrol gauge.

If you own multiple watches or prefer manual-wind pieces, an indicator is genuinely useful. For single-watch daily wearers, it's more decoration than necessity.

There's also a cost argument: adding an indicator means more components, more assembly complexity, and typically a higher price tag. Under $1,000, that money is often better spent on case finishing, movement grade, or sapphire crystal -- unless a power reserve indicator is something you'd personally enjoy checking.

For manual-wind watches specifically, the case for an indicator is stronger. On a manual winding watch, it shows the time left until the watch needs winding -- which matters when winding is entirely dependent on you remembering to do it.

## How does power reserve change if I rotate several watches?

This is the practical tipping point that most buyers don't think about until they already own two watches. When you wear one watch every day, power reserve rarely becomes an issue -- your wrist keeps it topped up. The moment you rotate, things change fast.

Say you wear Watch A Monday through Friday and put on Watch B over the weekend. If Watch A has a 41-hour reserve, it will stop sometime Saturday. You pick it up Monday and have to reset hours, minutes, date, and possibly day -- every single week. Multiply that across a modest three-watch rotation and the admin becomes genuinely annoying, especially on watches with complex calendars.

The practical floor for a rotating collection is around 65-70 hours. That gets a watch through a full 72-hour weekend with a buffer. For collectors who swap watches daily, this matters enough that a 70-hour Miyota 9039 or Powermatic 80-equipped piece often wins over a 38-hour NH35 variant -- even if the NH35 case finishing is superior. It's a real trade-off with no universal right answer, which is exactly why checking a watch against your existing collection habits before buying makes sense. ## Manual-wind vs. automatic: does power reserve work differently?

*A question we hear often:* buyers assume manual-wind watches are old-fashioned relics, but they're actually having a quiet moment in the microbrand world right now.

The fundamental mechanics are the same -- mainspring, barrel, escapement -- but the source of winding differs. Automatic watches house an oscillating weight that continues to power the watch with the movement of the wearer's wrist. Manual watches have no rotor; you wind them yourself each day via the crown.

Because manual-wind movements have no rotor, they can be made significantly thinner, which is one reason you see them in dress watches and some microbrands targeting slim profiles. It's recommended that all manual watches should be wound once a day. That daily ritual is either a meditative pleasure or an annoying chore depending on your personality, and it's worth being honest with yourself about which camp you fall into before buying a manual-wind piece.

Power reserve on a manual is arguably *more* critical than on an automatic, since there's no passive winding to top you up throughout the day. If you forget to wind for two days and the reserve runs out, you're setting the time from scratch.

At that frequency, the seconds hand sweeps smoothly and the movement handles positional variation better than slower-beat alternatives, which is part of why it's found in watches from affordable Swiss microbrands up through Oris and Sinn.

## What should I actually do with this information before buying?

Three practical questions to answer before power reserve becomes a factor in your next purchase:

1. **How many watches do you currently own and rotate?** One watch: 38-48 hours is fine. Two or more: aim for 65+ hours on anything that might sit for a weekend.
2. **Do you work a desk job with minimal arm movement?** If yes, your automatic may not wind efficiently through the day alone. Either choose a watch with a higher reserve as a buffer, or plan to top it up manually each morning.
3. **Is the watch you're considering a manual-wind?** If so, treat the power reserve number as a hard deadline and be realistic about whether you'll remember to wind daily.
