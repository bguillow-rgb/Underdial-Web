When you flip a watch over and see an exhibition caseback, or squint at a spec sheet that says "Swiss automatic movement," a reasonable question follows: *whose* movement is it, exactly, and does it change whether you should buy the watch? Most articles skip this entirely or bury it in jargon. This one doesn't.

The short answer: the movement brand matters for repairability, parts availability, and the accuracy class you can realistically expect. It matters less for whether the watch will keep running reliably through normal daily wear. Here's what each major supplier actually means for buyers under $1,000.

---

## Why do so many watches hide which movement they use?

*A question we hear often:*

Keeping movement suppliers confidential is a Swiss tradition. In the old "etablissage" system of cottage industries, only the watch company earned the right to label their product, and contractors were expected to keep quiet about their clientele. That's why a watch costing $600 might simply say "Swiss automatic" and leave the calibre number off the marketing materials entirely.

Today, movements are either made in-house or sourced from suppliers. More affordable brands still rely on mass-produced movements, and three manufacturers make the majority: ETA, Miyota, and Ronda.

For buyers in the under-$1,000 space, this is actually good news. The same proven, widely-serviced calibre can power a $250 microbrand and a $900 Swiss-made piece. Once you know the underlying movement, you can evaluate the watch on design, finishing, fit, and value, not on mystery engineering.

---

## What actually is ETA, and why does everyone mention it?

ETA is Switzerland's largest movement maker, and countless brands have relied on them since 1859. As a division of the Swatch Group, ETA supplies movements at mass-market volumes with consistent accuracy, which made them the default choice for established Swiss brands for decades.

The ETA 2824-2 built the reputation. ETA supplied movements to almost every major Swiss brand, including Tissot, Longines, Omega, and TAG Heuer. It's an automatic workhorse known for accuracy, durability, and easy serviceability.

Here's the wrinkle for modern buyers: since January 1, 2020, ETA stopped supplying mechanical movements to third-party customers, following a decision by the Swiss Competition Commission (COMCO) to reduce market concentration. ETA now supplies movements only to its own brands, Swatch, Omega, and Longines, though it still offers some quartz calibres to outside buyers.

The practical takeaway: if you find a non-Swatch Group Swiss watch today labelled "ETA inside," it was likely made before the cutoff or uses leftover inventory. New Swiss watches from independent brands now almost universally run Sellita.

---

## So what is Sellita, and is it actually the same thing?

*This one comes up a lot:*

Most Swiss watches under $2,000 share the same secret. Flip one over, and there's a strong chance a Sellita SW200 is ticking inside. When ETA began restricting movement sales to non-Swatch Group brands in the early 2000s, Sellita stepped up with a near-identical calibre. The SW200 now powers watches from Oris, Christopher Ward, TAG Heuer, and dozens more.

Since its launch in 2003, the Sellita SW200-1 has reached annual production of around 800,000 units. That's not a niche alternative. It's the de facto engine of the affordable Swiss watch industry.

The SW200-1 is a 25.6 mm, 26-jewel automatic running at 28,800 vph with 38 to 41 hours of power reserve, spec-for-spec comparable to the ETA it replaced. The four accuracy grades are worth knowing:

Standard grade adjusts in two positions with an accuracy of +/-12 to +/-30 seconds per day. Special (Elabore) adjusts in three positions with +/-7 to +/-20 seconds per day. Premium adjusts in five positions with +/-4 to +/-15 seconds per day. Chronometer meets COSC certification at -4 to +6 seconds per day.

The biggest real-world difference between the SW200 and ETA 2824-2 is price and availability at the manufacturer level, not at the consumer level. There is no meaningful price gap for the person buying the watch. The SW200 is simply the easier movement to source now that ETA prioritizes Swatch Group brands. For the person wearing the watch, the distinction is negligible.

One recent development worth noting: Sellita released the SW200-2 Power+ in early 2026, boosting power reserve to 65 hours while keeping full dimensional compatibility with the original. A meaningful upgrade if you rotate several watches.

## How does Miyota compare -- is it actually reliable?

Miyota, a subsidiary of Citizen since 1959, is one of the world's largest movement manufacturers. It's widely used by brands including Fossil, Orient, and Casio, and its reputation rests on cost-effectiveness and dependability.

The Miyota 8215 is the budget workhorse powering entry-level automatics under $200. The step-up 9015 is the one that changes the comparison. The 9015 runs at 28,800 vph, offers 42 hours of power reserve, and carries a similar jewel count to the ETA 2824-2, making it a genuine alternative rather than a consolation prize.

The key trade-off: Miyota movements are Japanese-made and don't carry the "Swiss Made" label, but they are often finished to a higher practical standard at the same price point. The 9015's slimmer profile also enables case designs that the chunkier Sellita SW200 simply can't match.

## What about Seiko's NH35 -- the movement every microbrand uses?

*Readers frequently ask:*

Wade into the microbrand world, and it'll be roughly two minutes before you encounter the Seiko NH35A. Called the "4R35" when used by Seiko itself, the NH35A is an automatic three-hand movement with a date function, a 3 Hz beat rate, a 41-hour power reserve, bi-directional winding, hand-winding, and hacking.

Seiko's subsidiary Time Module Inc. (TMI) distributes Seiko movements to third parties under separate designations, and the NH35 is their bestseller. It's cost-effective and complete: date, stop-seconds, and manual-winding in a package that shows up reliably in watches from $100 to $500.

Orient is a special case here. Though owned by Seiko, Orient produces its own in-house movements, known for affordability and solid construction. That makes Orient one of the only brands under $500 offering a genuine manufacture calibre, with all the brand accountability that implies.

## Does any of this actually affect which watch I should buy?

Here's the honest picture for buyers spending real money under $1,000.

| Movement | Origin | Beat Rate | Power Reserve | Accuracy Class | Typical Watch Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ETA 2824-2 | Swiss | 28,800 vph | 38-42 hr | +/-5-30 s/day (by grade) | $600-$2,000+ |
| Sellita SW200-1 | Swiss | 28,800 vph | 38-41 hr (65 hr in new Power+) | +/-6-30 s/day (by grade) | $400-$1,500 |
| Miyota 9015 | Japanese | 28,800 vph | 42 hr | +/-10-15 s/day | $200-$900 |
| Miyota 8215 | Japanese | 21,600 vph | 40 hr | +/-10-20 s/day | $100-$400 |
| Seiko NH35 (TMI) | Japanese | 21,600 vph | 41 hr | +/-10-15 s/day | $100-$500 |
| Orient F6922 / in-house | Japanese | 21,600 vph | 40 hr | +/-10-15 s/day | $150-$400 |
| Seagull ST series | Chinese | 21,600 vph | 38-42 hr | +/-15-30 s/day | $50-$300 |

Spending more doesn't buy dramatically better timekeeping. A $600 Tissot keeps time about as well as a $3,000 Tudor, and both use quality steel, sapphire, and similar gasket systems.

Movement brand matters most in three specific scenarios.

**1. Long-term serviceability.** Service intervals for Swiss automatic movements typically fall between three and five years, and you want a watchmaker who knows the calibre. ETA and Sellita have the widest pool of familiar independent watchmakers globally. Seiko NH-series movements are the cheapest and simplest to service anywhere on earth.

**2. Parts availability.** The shared architecture between the Sellita SW200 and ETA-style movements means watchmakers already understand the regulation and service process, and parts are globally stocked. Miyota parts are similarly abundant. Seagull and off-brand Chinese movements can be harder to source parts for outside major cities.

**3. Accuracy expectations.** If you need better than +/-10 seconds per day, look for a COSC-certified movement or a high-grade Sellita or ETA, not a standard NH35 or Miyota 8215. If +/-15 seconds per day is fine (it is for most people), all of the Japanese movements above deliver reliably.

Ultimately it comes down to the buyer and how much they're willing to spend. Plenty of respected brands don't make their own movements and still deliver high-quality timepieces.

## How do I actually find out what movement is in a watch I'm considering?

*A question we hear often:*

Three practical methods work reliably:

1. **Brand spec pages.** Serious brands, Oris, Christopher Ward, Hamilton, Baltic, publish the calibre name openly. If a brand hides this, that itself is useful information.
2. **Watch databases.** Sites like Watchbase and Caliber Corner index thousands of references and list the underlying calibre, including which movement a branded calibre is based on.
3. **Exhibition caseback.** If the watch has a display caseback, movement makers stamp their company seal and calibre number on the plate under the balance wheel. A 10x loupe usually answers the question on the spot.

Underdial, the iOS watch-advisor app, surfaces the movement type when you scan a watch, so you can check at a glance whether the calibre inside fits your standards before committing. It's particularly useful when you're comparing two watches at similar prices and want to see if one's movement tier actually justifies the premium.

## FAQ

**Is a Sellita movement as good as an ETA?**
Yes, for daily wear purposes. The Sellita SW200 is dimensionally identical to the ETA 2824-2, runs at the same 28,800 vph, and is used by respected brands like Oris and Christopher Ward. The practical differences in accuracy and durability are negligible for most buyers.

**Why do some watches say "Swiss movement" but don't name ETA or Sellita?**
Keeping suppliers confidential is a Swiss tradition from the old etablissage cottage-industry system, where only the watch company earned the right to label their product. The brand name on the rotor is often just the watch company's logo over a supplied Sellita, ETA, or Miyota calibre. Check the spec sheet or Watchbase for the underlying calibre.

**Is the NH35 a good movement for an everyday watch?**
Yes. The Seiko NH35A is an automatic three-hand movement with hand-winding, hacking, and a 41-hour power reserve. It's reliable, widely serviced, and found in hundreds of watches from $100 to $500. Its main limitation is a modest accuracy of roughly +/-10-15 seconds per day.

**What does "in-house movement" mean and does it matter under $1,000?**
An in-house movement is designed and manufactured by the watch brand itself. Orient, though owned by Seiko, produces its own in-house movements, known for affordability and solid construction, and they power many entry-level automatic watches. Below $1,000, Seiko and Orient are the main brands offering genuine in-house calibres. The practical benefit is brand accountability; third-party movements like Miyota or Sellita are often easier to service independently.

**Which movement supplier is best for serviceability?**
ETA and Sellita have the widest independent watchmaker familiarity in the Swiss world. Seiko NH-series movements are the easiest and cheapest to service globally. Miyota 8215/9015 movements are widely stocked and competitively priced to service. If long-term repairability in any city is your priority, the NH35 or Miyota 9015 are hard to beat.
