Watch Strap Types: How to Choose the Right One

By Underdial · Published July 1, 2026

Quick Answer

The five main watch strap types are leather, NATO/nylon, rubber, metal bracelet, and mesh/Milanese. Each suits a different context: leather for dress and casual wear, NATO for travel and sport, rubber for water and active use, metal for durability and formality. Lug width, typically 18-22 mm, determines compatibility.

Most people spend hundreds of dollars on a watch case and movement, then wear the strap it came with indefinitely. That’s a missed opportunity. A $15 NATO swap can turn a dive watch from boardroom-adjacent to beach-ready in 30 seconds. Understanding why different materials behave so differently is the key to getting more from any watch you already own, or buying smarter the next time.

What exactly are the different watch strap types?

There are five main categories you’ll encounter under $1,000:

  1. Leather, the traditional choice, from dress to casual
  2. NATO / Nylon, single-piece pass-through straps with military roots
  3. Rubber / FKM, water-resistant and sport-oriented
  4. Metal bracelet, linked steel or titanium, the most durable option
  5. Mesh / Milanese, woven steel links, sits between bracelet and strap in feel

Every category has sub-types worth knowing (Zulu, Perlon, Bund, sailcloth), but those five cover nearly every buying decision you’ll face. A watch strap is the bracelet that attaches a wrist watch to the wrist, and straps may be made of leather, plastic, polyurethane, silicone, rubber, FKM, cloth, or metal, sometimes in combination.

Before getting into each type, one rule applies universally: lug width must match. Your wrist size and the watch’s lug width directly influence strap compatibility, always measure your current strap or verify your watch’s specifications before purchasing replacements. A 20 mm strap simply won’t fit an 18 mm watch, regardless of how appealing it appears. The most common lug widths on affordable watches are 18, 20, and 22 mm, check your watch’s caseback or spec sheet before ordering anything.

What’s the right strap if I want one that works everywhere?

A question we hear often:

If you genuinely want one strap that handles office Monday and trail Saturday, leather is the conventional answer, but it’s a compromise in both directions. A well-crafted leather strap transitions effortlessly from business meetings to dinner dates, while NATO straps shine during weekend adventures or travel where ruggedness takes priority.

The honest answer for 2026 is that FKM rubber has closed the gap considerably. FKM rubber handles the widest range of dual-wear conditions, active, humid, professional, without deforming or cracking. For formal contexts, full-grain calfskin or cordovan leather performs well if conditioned regularly and kept away from sustained moisture. If your life involves commuting, occasional gym stops, and weekend hiking, a high-quality FKM rubber strap does more work than leather without the water anxiety.

That said, leather still communicates something rubber doesn’t: warmth, tradition, a willingness to slow down. On a Seiko SPB or an Orient Bambino, brown leather reads completely differently from the same case on rubber, not better or worse, but unmistakably different.

What makes a NATO strap different from other nylon straps?

NATO straps emerged from military necessity in the 1970s, originally issued to British Ministry of Defense personnel as standardized watch bands. The key engineering detail is the pass-through design: a single piece of nylon threads under the watch case and over both spring bars, so if one spring bar fails, the watch doesn’t fall off your wrist. The advantage of a NATO watch strap is its durability and resilience, they’re typically made of woven nylon that can handle any environment and won’t easily break or cut. They also provide additional security due to the double strap method: if one spring bar snaps or falls out, the watch is still retained by the other.

A closely related option is the Zulu strap. The Zulu strap is very similar to the NATO but has a thicker nylon weave, thicker and more rounded buckles and keepers compared to the rectangular ones common to the NATO, and a higher level of robustness in general. For most buyers under $1,000, the standard NATO is the better starting point, lower profile, easier to find in quality colorways, and plenty robust for everyday use.

In 2026, a newer variant has grown popular too. The single-pass NATO has become a massive trend, addressing the common critique that traditional NATO straps add too much bulk beneath the watch. This design eliminates the secondary under-strap, allowing the watch case to sit closer to the wrist while still providing the continuous-loop security of nylon, and offers a cleaner, more streamlined aesthetic that pairs well with thinner watches. Worth considering if you run a 40 mm or smaller case.

The term ‘strap’ typically refers to a band made of softer materials like leather, rubber, or fabric, while a ‘bracelet’ is made from a series of linked pieces of metal. In practical terms, metal bracelets can be heavier than some people might prefer for daily wear and require tools for adjustment, wrists fluctuate slightly in diameter over the course of the day, which is why a watch sometimes feels tighter or looser, and a strap is easier to adjust for comfort in such scenarios.

For most buyers in the Seiko 5, Citizen Promaster, or Tissot PRX range: if the watch ships on a bracelet and you like the look, keep it there. Buy one quality NATO or leather strap for variety. That’s a more useful investment than a third watch.

What’s the difference between rubber and FKM, does it matter?

This one comes up a lot:

Yes, it matters more than the marketing suggests. Standard silicone straps are the softest and cheapest option, fine for casual wear, but they tend to feel sticky in heat and can degrade with UV exposure over time. FKM (fluorocarbon rubber), used by brands like Seiko on their higher-spec Prospex models and by aftermarket strap makers, is significantly more durable. Silicone is highly flexible, completely waterproof, and resistant to sweat and UV rays, making it the go-to for intense workouts, swimming, and outdoor sports, and modern silicone blends are hypoallergenic and feature a soft-touch finish that prevents dust accumulation.

FKM handles the harder end of that spectrum: chemical exposure, extreme temperature ranges, prolonged immersion. That’s why it’s the standard material for serious dive and sport watches. If your Seiko Prospex or Citizen Promaster comes on an FKM rubber strap, that’s a genuine spec advantage over a cheap silicone strap from a no-name supplier.

For serious diving and outdoor use, FKM rubber bands or titanium bracelets provide the maximum in terms of durability and water resistance when you’re going under the waves regularly or diving in difficult conditions.

What about mesh and Milanese straps, are they worth considering?

Mesh bracelets (often called Milanese, though technically a specific woven variant) occupy a useful middle ground. They feel lighter and more flexible than a solid-link bracelet, adjust with a sliding clasp rather than individual links, and have a distinctive fine-woven texture that reads dressier than NATO but less formal than polished steel. A watch with a stainless steel mesh bracelet, including the Milanese braided mesh variant, offers a distinct aesthetic that sits between a traditional strap and a solid bracelet.

For Tissot and Orient dress watches in the $150-$400 range, a 20 mm mesh bracelet from a reputable aftermarket maker (Hirsch, Strapcode, DaLuca) typically runs $30-$60 and transforms the wrist presence of a dress watch considerably. Worth experimenting with if you’ve been defaulting to leather.

How do I actually match a strap to the type of watch I own?

Watch archetype and strap type interact in ways that can look obviously right or subtly wrong. A few practical rules:

Dive watches pair naturally with rubber, NATO, or metal bracelet. Leather straps aren’t typically waterproof and require careful maintenance, which is why they’re generally not used with dive watches, since the pairing is counter-intuitive. That said, a brown leather strap on a vintage-style diver (think Seiko SRPD or Orient Kamasu) is a legitimate aesthetic choice as long as you’re not actually diving.

Field and pilot watches are natural NATO territory. NATO straps emerged from military necessity in the 1970s, originally issued to British Ministry of Defense personnel, and the visual language of canvas, nylon, and olive drab still reads authentically on tool watches. A canvas or woven NATO on a Seiko 5 Sports or an Orient Defender looks intentional, not accidental.

Dress watches belong on leather or mesh. Leather straps are soft to the touch and generally signify a watch worn in formal circumstances. A slim, double-stitched calfskin strap in tan or tobacco brown makes a Tissot Le Locle or Orient Bambino look considerably more considered than the stock bracelet it ships with.

Sport chronographs work on rubber or bracelet. The pushers and rotating bezel of a chrono suggest active use, leather can work for dress-up occasions, but rubber or a solid bracelet is the natural home.

Underdial can help you check a strap purchase against what you already own before you order something that clashes with your box. If you’re eyeing a second watch purely because you’re bored with your current one, a strap swap is almost always the smarter first move.

How much should I actually spend on a replacement strap?

Price tiers exist and the quality jump between them is real:

  • $8-$20: Mass-market NATO and silicone straps. Acceptable for experimentation, but hardware quality (buckles, spring bars) is often poor.
  • $20-$50: The sweet spot for leather and FKM rubber. Brands like Hirsch, Barton, and DaLuca sit here. Stitching, lining, and clasp quality are meaningfully better.
  • $50-$120: Premium leather (Horween shell cordovan, Italian calfskin), high-spec FKM rubber, and quality mesh bracelets. Justified if you wear one watch daily and hate replacing straps.
  • $120+: Exotic leathers, bespoke makers, deployment clasps in solid steel. Beyond the needs of most buyers under $1,000.

A good rule: don’t spend more than 15%-20% of the watch’s value on a strap. A $50 leather strap on a $200 Orient is proportionate. A $100 strap on a $120 Seiko 5 is not.

NATO straps are the easiest type to swap in and out, requiring no additional tools, and are probably the strap that can most ideally be customized to fit an individual’s wrist, if there’s any extra slack once tightened, you can just loop the tail end back into one of the keepers. That accessibility is exactly why they’re the right starting point for anyone new to strap swapping.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is lug width and why does it matter for straps?

Lug width is the gap between your watch's lugs, the 'ears' that hold the spring bars. It's measured in millimeters (most commonly 18, 19, 20, or 22 mm) and must match your strap exactly. A 20 mm strap won't fit an 18 mm watch. Check the caseback stamp or your watch's spec sheet; many manufacturers print the lug width directly on the existing strap.

How long does a leather watch strap last?

A quality leather strap worn daily typically lasts 1-3 years before the inside lining cracks or the holes wear out. Sweat and water shorten its life significantly. Rotating two leather straps extends each one, and conditioning with leather balm every few months slows drying. Budget straps use bonded leather and may last only a few months.

Can I put any strap on any watch?

Not always. Standard spring-bar watches accept any strap of the correct lug width. However, watches with integrated bracelets (like many sport and fashion models) only accept brand-specific bands. Some dive watches also have fixed strap bars, accepting only single-pass NATO straps. Always check whether your watch has standard or proprietary lug attachment before buying a replacement strap.

What is the difference between NATO and Zulu straps?

Both are single-piece pass-through nylon straps, but Zulu straps use heavier-gauge nylon weave and thicker, more rounded buckles and keepers, making them bulkier and more robust. NATO straps use flatter, rectangular hardware and thinner nylon, giving them a lower profile on the wrist. For everyday wear, NATO is the slimmer, more popular option.

Is a metal bracelet or a strap better for everyday wear?

Metal bracelets are more durable, they don't wear out the way leather or nylon do, but they're heavier and can feel warm in hot weather. Straps are lighter, more comfortable in heat, and easier to resize on the fly. For true daily rotation, many buyers keep the watch on its metal bracelet and own one leather or NATO strap for weekends and travel.

What's the cheapest way to refresh the look of a watch I already own?

A new strap is by far the most affordable transformation. A quality NATO nylon strap costs $10-$20 and takes seconds to swap without tools. A decent leather strap runs $20-$50. The case, dial, and movement stay identical, but the entire character of the watch shifts. It's the single highest-impact, lowest-cost upgrade in the hobby.
