Watch Jewels Explained: What That Number Really Means

By Underdial · Published July 14, 2026

Quick Answer

Watch jewels are tiny synthetic ruby or sapphire bearings placed at friction points inside a mechanical movement, not gemstones for show. A 17-jewel movement covers every critical pivot in a basic watch. More jewels indicate more complexity (auto winding, complications), not automatically higher quality. Quartz watches need almost none.

You’re looking at a watch listing, Seiko, Orient, or maybe a Swiss microbrand, and right there in the specs it says “21 jewels.” Or sometimes “17 jewels.” Occasionally “24 jewels.” Nobody explains it. The listing moves on to water resistance. You’re left wondering whether more is better, whether these are actual gemstones, and whether the number means anything real for a watch under $1,000.

It means something. But probably not what you think.

Wait, are those real gems inside my watch?

A question we hear often:

The jewels in a watch are tiny pieces of synthetic ruby or sapphire built into the movement. Their purpose is to provide a hard, smooth surface for pivots and other moving parts, not to add sparkle to the dial.

Watchmakers originally relied on natural rubies and sapphires until Auguste Verneuil developed synthetic alternatives in 1902. That breakthrough made precision timekeeping far more affordable, allowing movement jewels to be manufactured consistently rather than sourced from increasingly scarce natural stones.

So yes, genuine synthetic gemstones, engineered for function, not aesthetics. Both synthetic sapphire and ruby rate a 9 on the Mohs hardness scale, second only to diamonds. That hardness is the whole point. Metal-on-metal at microscopic pivot points creates friction, heat, and wear. A near-diamond-hard synthetic bearing running against a polished metal shaft loses almost nothing over years of use.

What does the jewel count actually tell me?

17 jewels form the backbone of any reliable time-only movement, covering every critical friction point: the mainspring barrel, the complete gear train (center, third, and fourth wheels), the escape wheel, pallet fork, and both balance wheel pivots. Skip any of these and you’ll feel the difference in timekeeping accuracy within months of regular wear.

That’s why 17 is the number you’ll encounter on countless respected movements. It’s generally considered the standard for a high-quality manual-wind watch.

Go above 17 and the extra jewels are doing extra work, literally. An automatic watch has a self-winding rotor and associated gearing, all of which need their own bearings, so automatic movements typically carry more jewels than manual ones, often 21, 23, 25 or more. Watches with complications like a date, day-date, or chronograph need additional jewels for the extra parts.

Jewel count is a proxy for complexity, not quality. A 24-jewel automatic is not necessarily better than a 17-jewel manual. It simply has more going on inside.

So what do 17, 21, and 25 jewels each mean in practice?

Here’s how the standard counts map to what’s actually happening inside the movement:

Most watches you’d buy under $1,000 live comfortably in the 17-25 range, covering everything from a time-only Seiko 5 to a day-date Orient automatic.

Does a higher jewel count mean better accuracy?

This one comes up a lot:

No. This is the single most important thing to understand before you let a jewel count influence a purchase. Accuracy in a mechanical movement comes down to how well the hairspring is regulated, how many positions the movement was adjusted in, and the quality of the escapement. None of those are jewel-count questions.

A well-executed 17-jewel movement from a respected manufacturer often outperforms a hastily assembled 25-jewel one with poor finishing or inferior materials. Bearing tolerances, installation quality, and overall regulation matter far more than the number on the dial.

This is exactly where ETA’s movement grade system matters to buyers, and it’s something most listings bury or ignore entirely. The same ETA caliber comes in four grades (Standard, Elaboré, Top, Chronometer) with accuracy ranging from +/-12 sec/day to COSC-certified -4/+6 sec/day. All four grades share the same jewel count. A Standard-grade ETA 2824-2 and a Chronometer-grade ETA 2824-2 have identical jewel counts. The grade difference is in regulation, hairspring material, and positional adjustment, things that don’t print on the dial but matter enormously for daily accuracy.

Standard grade is adjusted in two positions, with an average rate of +/-12 seconds per day. Elaboré grade is adjusted in three positions, averaging +/-7 seconds per day. Top grade can be adjusted in five positions, averaging +/-4 seconds per day.

Most brands don’t advertise which ETA grade they use. A $1,200 watch might use a Standard-grade ETA 2824-2 in a premium case, while an $800 watch from another brand might use Elaboré. If you’re choosing between two Swiss-movement watches, it’s worth emailing the brand to ask which caliber and grade they use. Brands that take pride in their movements will answer readily. Brands that get evasive may have something to hide.

What about Japanese movements, do jewel counts work the same way?

Yes and no. The functional logic is identical (more moving parts need more jewels), but Japanese movement makers like Seiko and Miyota don’t use the tiered grade system ETA does.

The Seiko NH35, found in dozens of watches under $500, runs a 24-jewel automatic movement. The Miyota 9015, widely considered one of the better movements in its class, also runs 24 jewels. Both are regulated to roughly +/-10-15 seconds per day out of the box, with many examples performing significantly tighter.

ETA-powered watches typically start at $500 and up. Japanese movements like the NH35 and Miyota 9015 offer comparable daily performance at lower price points, and the gap between a Standard-grade ETA 2824-2 and a Seiko NH35 is narrower than the price difference suggests. For most everyday buyers under $1,000, the choice between them is more a question of Swiss heritage preference than real-world performance.

Is there ever such a thing as too many jewels?

Readers frequently ask:

Yes, historically, and it became a real problem. During the mid-twentieth century there was something of a jewel-count race, with some makers fitting non-functional jewels purely to print an impressive number on the dial. It became enough of an issue that standards were eventually introduced requiring only functional jewels to be counted.

Today those rules mean every jewel listed in a modern watch is doing actual work. That said, context still matters. Jewel count should be one piece of information, not the whole verdict. A well-made 17-jewel movement can be more desirable than a higher-count movement assembled carelessly.

Does any of this apply to quartz watches?

Very little. Quartz movements have far fewer mechanical moving parts, so they may use only one or two jewels, or none at all, and they keep time just fine either way.

If you’re looking at a quartz Tissot, Citizen Eco-Drive, or any solar-powered watch, ignore the jewel count entirely. What matters is accuracy, battery or power cell longevity, and brand-specific features like Citizen’s anti-magnetic technology. The jewel number, if listed at all, tells you essentially nothing about a quartz watch’s quality.

How do I use jewel count practically when buying a watch?

Use it as a quick sanity check, not a verdict. “17 jewels” on a manual-wind dress watch means a fully jeweled movement, no red flags. “21 jewels” on an automatic means the rotor and date mechanism are accounted for, also fine. A dramatically inflated number like “50 jewels” on an inexpensive vintage piece is a signal to dig deeper, because those extra jewels were almost certainly decorative.

What actually drives quality at the movement level is regulation, grade (for ETA and Sellita calibers), and the reputation of the manufacturer. COSC-certified Swiss-made calibers are always in demand, but Japanese movements from Seiko’s in-house division and well-established ETA-based calibers offer similar reliability at a lower price.

FAQs

What does “21 jewels” mean on a watch? It means the movement contains 21 synthetic ruby or sapphire bearings at friction points. The extra jewels beyond the baseline 17 typically support an automatic winding rotor and its reduction gears, or calendar functions, not decorative gems, and not a direct indicator of quality.

Is a 17-jewel watch good? Yes. Seventeen jewels is considered “fully jeweled” for a manually wound movement, it covers every critical friction point. A quality 17-jewel movement from Seiko, ETA, or Miyota can outperform a shoddily assembled 25-jewel movement any day.

Do more jewels make a watch more accurate? Not directly. Accuracy is determined by regulation, hairspring quality, beat rate, and positional adjustment, not jewel count. A well-regulated 17-jewel movement will beat a poorly regulated 25-jewel one every time.

Do quartz watches have jewels? Rarely, and when they do, it’s usually just one or two. Quartz movements have far fewer mechanical moving parts, so the friction-reducing role jewels play in mechanical watches is almost irrelevant. Don’t factor jewel count into a quartz purchase.

What are ETA movement grades, and do they relate to jewel count? ETA offers four grades, Standard, Elaboré, Top, and Chronometer, that differ in regulation quality, hairspring materials, and accuracy tolerances. Crucially, all four grades of the same caliber share the same jewel count. Grade affects precision, not gem count.
