Watch Size Guide: How to Find the Right Fit for Your Wrist

By Underdial · Published June 17, 2026

Quick Answer

Measure your wrist circumference with a soft tape, then match it to a lug-to-lug length no longer than about 75–80% of your wrist's flat top surface. Case diameter alone is misleading — lug-to-lug is the number that truly determines whether a watch sits well or overhangs uncomfortably.

Getting the size wrong is the single most common way people waste money on a watch they rarely reach for. They fall for a case diameter on a spec sheet, the watch arrives, and it either swamps their wrist or feels like a postage stamp. This guide gives you the real numbers — lug-to-lug, diameter, thickness, and wrist circumference — so you can size confidently before spending anything.

Why does everyone say “40mm” like it means something?

A question we hear often:

Because retailers put case diameter front and centre, it has become the shorthand for “size.” But diameter alone tells you very little about how a watch actually wears. On paper, sizing seems straightforward — you have a diameter and assume that’s enough to predict fit. In reality those numbers only tell a small part of the story. The way a watch sits on your wrist has as much to do with lug-to-lug, case shape, bracelet design, and wrist shape as it does the diameter on the spec sheet.

Two watches can share a 40mm case and behave completely differently on the same wrist. Two watches with the same 40mm case can have lug-to-lug measurements of, say, 47mm vs 51mm, and the longer one will overhang a 6.5-inch wrist while the shorter one fits perfectly. That four-millimetre gap is invisible on a product page and obvious the moment you put the watch on.

Okay — what IS lug-to-lug, exactly?

Lug-to-lug is the straight-line distance from the tip of the top lug to the tip of the bottom lug, measured across the case. It determines whether the watch sits flat on your wrist or overhangs the edges — diameter doesn’t. Think of it as the watch’s footprint: the number that governs whether the case hugs your wrist contour or seesaws across it.

Retailers don’t always provide the lug-to-lug measurement in a watch description, which is exactly the problem. A motivated buyer needs to look it up from a third-party database, a watch enthusiast forum, or a tool like Underdial, which surfaces lug-to-lug data alongside its fit verdict. Typical lug-to-lug ranges run 42–55mm for men’s watches and 36–46mm for women’s and unisex pieces.

One useful rule of thumb: lug-to-lug should be no more than 75 to 80 percent of the wrist diameter. Your wrist is not a flat shelf — it curves — so the usable flat span at the top is always shorter than the full circumference.

How do I actually measure my wrist?

This one comes up a lot:

Measure your wrist circumference with a flexible tape just above the wrist bone, where the watch sits. Pull the tape snug but not tight. Record to the nearest quarter inch or 5mm. No tape measure? A strip of paper, a pen mark, and a ruler work just as well.

The average wrist size for men is around 7.25 inches (18.5 cm), while for women it is approximately 6.5 inches (16.5 cm). These averages matter because most watch design targets them — if your wrists land at either extreme, you’ll need to filter more aggressively for lug-to-lug, not just diameter.

Once you have your circumference, use this rough lug-to-lug guide:

Wrist CircumferenceRecommended Lug-to-LugNotes
Under 6.0 in / 15 cm38–44 mmMany modern men’s watches won’t fit; prioritise compact dress pieces
6.0–6.5 in / 15–16.5 cm42–46 mmCheck lug-to-lug carefully; 40mm dials vary widely here
6.5–7.0 in / 16.5–18 cm44–48 mmSeiko 5 SRPD (47mm) is the textbook benchmark
7.0–7.5 in / 18–19 cm46–50 mmMost affordable automatics and divers land comfortably here
7.5 in+ / 19 cm+48–55 mmLarge sport watches, G-Shocks, and big-cushion divers become viable

One caveat worth knowing: curved lugs and cushion cases bend the rules. A 50mm lug-to-lug with curved lugs often wears like a 47mm flat-lug watch because the lugs follow the wrist contour. Numbers are a guide, not gospel.

What case diameter should I actually look for?

Readers frequently ask:

Diameter still matters — it governs visual weight and dial legibility, and it’s the first filter most people apply. The average men’s watch case size ranges from 38–46mm, while the average women’s watch case size falls between 26–38mm. A pairing worth knowing: for a wrist under 6 inches, a case diameter of 28–36mm is typical; for a 6–7-inch wrist, 36–40mm; for a 7–8-inch wrist, 40–44mm; and above 8 inches, 44mm and up tend to look proportional.

Historically, men’s watch sizes have shifted a great deal. 36mm was the standard men’s dress watch size from the 1950s through the 1990s and has returned to favour in recent years. On a 6.5 to 7.5-inch wrist, a 36mm dress watch reads as classic, vintage-inspired, and intentional. The oversized trend of the 2000s pushed the average up to 42–44mm, but the pendulum has swung back. For 2026 buyers, this means the sub-39mm market is richer in quality options than it was just five years ago — brands like Tissot, Orient, and Baltic all have strong offerings in that range well under $1,000.

When in doubt, prioritise the lug-to-lug check. Diameter is the headline spec. Lug-to-lug is where the story actually lives.

Does case thickness matter, or is it just a vanity number?

Thickness is the underrated third dimension, and it matters more than most first-time buyers expect. Case thickness is the depth of the watch from the back to the top of the crystal. Thicker watches tend to be sportier or more technical — like dive watches — while thinner watches feel dressier and more refined.

Practically speaking, anything over roughly 13–14mm starts to catch on shirt cuffs and feel present on the wrist in a way that not everyone enjoys. Automatic movements add thickness because they require space for the rotor; a typical affordable automatic like the Seiko NH35 adds roughly 2–3mm compared to an equivalent quartz. If you plan to wear a watch under a dress shirt regularly, a case thickness of 10–12mm is the sweet spot — and that’s entirely achievable under $500 from brands like Tissot (the PRX Automatic runs 11mm), Seiko Presage, and Hamilton Khaki.

Shopping for a sport watch or diver? Thickness matters less because those watches are expected to be substantial — and watch thickness adds to the overall feel: thin reads more elegant, thick reads more rugged. The main concern for divers is whether the case clears the sleeve of a wetsuit, which is a different problem entirely.

I keep seeing photos online where the watch looks massive, but reviewers say it wears small. What’s going on?

Macro photography compresses depth and amplifies the watch face relative to the wrist. A watch that looks correctly proportioned in real life appears oversized in close-up photos — and the opposite is also true: watches that look correct in product photography often look small in person. Trust how a watch looks on your wrist in a mirror, not how it looks in lifestyle photos online.

This is one reason wrist-shot communities on Reddit’s r/Watches and r/Seiko are so valuable — real people photograph the same watch on a range of wrist sizes, giving you a realistic sense of proportion before you commit. What about strap width — does that affect fit too?

A question we hear often:

Lug width is the internal measurement between the two lugs of a watch — the measurement that determines what strap size you’d buy. If you have a watch with a 20mm lug width, you need a 20mm strap. Getting this wrong means straps won’t seat properly in the spring-bar holes, and the watch will look misaligned.

Lug widths range from 6mm up to 30mm, with the most common sizes between 16mm and 24mm. In modern watches, 18mm, 20mm, and 22mm are the most popular. One rule of thumb: strap width is typically about half the case diameter — a 40mm case usually takes a 20mm strap, a 44mm case often takes a 22mm strap. Deviating from that proportion (say, putting a 24mm strap on a 38mm case) tends to look heavy and unbalanced.

For buyers building a first collection under $1,000, sticking to 20mm lug-width watches gives you the widest aftermarket strap selection at the lowest prices — NATO straps, leather, rubber, and mesh are all easy to find in 20mm from makers like Erika’s Originals or WatchGecko without spending more than $20–40.

Quick reference: how do I put this all together before I buy?

Here’s the three-step process our editorial team uses every time before recommending a watch:

  1. Measure your wrist circumference — use a tape above the wrist bone.
  2. Look up the lug-to-lug, not just the diameter — check the brand’s spec sheet, a watch database, or Underdial’s model data.
  3. Compare to your wrist’s flat span — lug-to-lug should stay at or below roughly 75–80% of your wrist diameter (circumference ÷ π ≈ diameter).

If the lug-to-lug clears that threshold, check thickness for sleeve clearance and strap width for aftermarket flexibility. That three-point check takes about two minutes and will prevent the most common regret in the sub-$1,000 watch market: a beautiful watch you never reach for because it doesn’t fit right.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I measure my wrist for a watch?

Wrap a flexible tape measure snugly just above your wrist bone — where the watch will actually sit — and note the circumference in inches or millimetres. A strip of paper and a ruler works just as well if you don't have a tape. The average male wrist runs 7.0–7.25 inches; the average female wrist is around 6.0–6.5 inches.

What's the difference between case diameter and lug-to-lug?

Case diameter is the width of the watch face, measured across the dial and excluding the crown. Lug-to-lug is the tip-to-tip distance from the top lug to the bottom lug — the actual footprint the watch places on your wrist. Two watches with identical 40mm diameters can have lug-to-lug measurements 4–5mm apart, making one overhang on a smaller wrist while the other fits perfectly.

What lug-to-lug length is right for a 7-inch wrist?

A 7-inch wrist has roughly 52–54mm of flat surface across the top. Aim for a lug-to-lug in the 46–50mm range for a comfortable, proportional fit. The Seiko 5 SRPD series (47mm lug-to-lug) is a widely cited benchmark for this wrist size.

Is a 40mm watch too big for a small wrist?

It depends more on lug-to-lug than on diameter. A 40mm watch with a short, 46mm lug-to-lug will sit cleanly on a 6.5-inch wrist. A 40mm watch with a 52mm lug-to-lug will overhang the same wrist noticeably. Always check the lug-to-lug before buying, not just the headline diameter.

Does watch thickness matter for fit?

Yes, especially under shirt cuffs and for everyday comfort. A watch thicker than 13–14mm can feel bulky and won't slide easily under a dress-shirt sleeve. Sport and dive watches often run 13–16mm thick; slim dress watches typically stay under 10mm. As a rule, thinner reads dressier, thicker reads sportier.
